What a City Average Hides

The surprising truth about the air we breathe, and why one number can never tell the full story.

Every day, people in cities like Dubai, Delhi, and Dhaka check air-quality apps and see a simple label. But a single average hides the timing, the bad days, the particle mix, and the weather conditions behind the number.

In this project, we analysed air quality and weather across selected global cities to uncover what is behind this average.

What we found is simple: cities do not only differ in how polluted they are. They differ in when pollution appears, how often people live through unhealthy days, and what kind of particle profile shapes the air.

01 · Data journey

From public sensors to a story-ready dataset.

We started with more than 1.5 million raw OpenAQ pollutant records from 18 candidate cities. After checking coverage and data quality, we kept 15 cities for the main 2025 comparison and enriched the air-quality data with Open-Meteo weather features.

OpenAQ

PM2.5 and PM10 records were cleaned and reshaped into city-hourly, daily, and monthly datasets.

Open-Meteo

Weather features such as wind speed, temperature, humidity, cloud cover, and precipitation were joined to the data.

Coverage audit

We removed cities with weak 2025 coverage, so the final comparison is based on more reliable city data.

01
Global view

Where is the air thickest, and when?

Watch how the world breathe month by month. Each circle shows a city’s monthly median PM2.5 in 2025. Color shows pollution burden and marker size reflects active monitoring coverage.

What to notice: The map shows where monthly PM2.5 is highest in 2025. South Asian cities appear darker more often, while developed-city references stay lighter and more stable. Marker size reflects active monitoring coverage, not city population.

02
Seasonality fingerprint

Every city has its own dangerous season.

A yearly average hides the rhythm of pollution. The heatmap shows which cities stay high throughout the year and which cities rise or fall by season.

What to notice: The heatmap shows that pollution has a seasonal rhythm. Delhi, Dhaka and Lahore have several high-PM2.5 months, while New York, London, Tokyo and Los Angeles stay much lower.

03
Ranking transitions

The ranking changes every month.

Yearly averages create a false sense of a fixed winner. The bump chart shows how city positions shift month by month.

The most polluted city depends on when you look.

What to notice: The bump chart shows that city rankings change month by month. This is why a single annual ranking cannot fully describe how people experience pollution during the year.

04
Lived burden

How many days is the air actually unhealthy?

A yearly average can hide repeated exposure. The dot matrix asks a more human question: how often do people breathe air above the WHO daily PM2.5 guideline?

What to notice: Red days are above the WHO daily PM2.5 guideline. Delhi, Lahore and Dhaka have the highest exceedance burden, while cities such as New York, Tokyo and London have many more below-guideline days.

05
Particle profile

Not all pollution is the same.

PM2.5 and PM10 describe different particle-size ranges. The PM2.5 to PM10 ratio helps compare particle profiles, but it does not prove exact sources.

What to notice: The ridgeline plot compares PM2.5 / PM10 ratios. A higher ratio suggests a more fine-particle-dominated profile, while a lower ratio suggests more PM10-heavy conditions.

06
Weather episodes

When the wind changes, pollution episodes change too.

Pollution never happens in isolation. Wind speed, PM10, and particle ratio help show how weather context separates different kinds of bad-air days.

What to notice: Each point is one city-day. The chart shows that high-pollution days can happen under different wind and particle-profile conditions across cities.

07
City fingerprints

Every city has its own pollution fingerprint.

The fingerprint map combines seasonality, exceedance burden, particle profile, and weather context to compare cities by behavior, not only by pollution level.

What to notice: Cities close together have similar pollution fingerprints. Delhi, Dhaka and Lahore form a high-burden cluster, while Dubai and Riyadh separate as a different Gulf-city pattern.

Final takeaways

Three things every city average hides.

Rankings are unstable.

The “most polluted city” changes across months. Annual rankings hide seasonal peaks and short pollution episodes.

Exposure matters more than averages.

The number of above-guideline days shows the lived burden more clearly than one yearly mean.

Cities have fingerprints.

PM2.5 burden, PM10 context, weather, seasonality, and daily episodes create distinct city profiles.

The air we breathe is not just data.

It is a daily story of health, timing, place, and exposure. Understanding what a city average hides is the first step toward safe air and better quality of life.

Limitations

The analysis is descriptive and coverage-aware. PM2.5 to PM10 ratios are interpreted as particle-profile indicators, not as direct source attribution. Sensor coverage differs by city, and source confirmation would require additional land-use, emissions, or source-apportionment data.
