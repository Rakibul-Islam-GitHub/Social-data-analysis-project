Global urban air story

What a City Average Hides

We followed PM2.5, PM10, and weather across selected global cities to ask a simple question: when people say one city is more polluted than another, what patterns disappear behind that average?

01 · Data journey

From public sensors to a story-ready dataset.

We combined OpenAQ air-quality measurements with Open-Meteo weather variables, then filtered cities using data coverage. This means the story starts with a methodological choice: compare only cities with enough usable evidence.

OpenAQ

PM2.5 and PM10 observations were fetched from the OpenAQ S3 archive, aggregated from location-hour to city-hour, then city-day and city-month.

Open-Meteo

Weather features such as wind speed, temperature, humidity, cloud cover, and precipitation were joined to city-hourly data.

Coverage audit

Cities with weak or misaligned coverage were excluded from the main web story and retained as documented context.

Global view

Where does PM2.5 concentrate across the year?

The animated map shows monthly median PM2.5 across selected cities. Marker size reflects active monitoring coverage, while color shows the pollution burden in that month.

Figure: Animated monthly map of median PM2.5. The map introduces the global geography of the story before we look inside the year.

02
Seasonality fingerprint

The same city can look different by month.

A yearly average hides the rhythm of pollution. The heatmap reveals which cities stay high throughout the year and which ones rise or fall seasonally.

03
Ranking transitions

Rankings move. Exposure does not wait for annual averages.

The bump chart ranks cities month by month using median PM2.5. It shows why a static “most polluted city” list is too simple.

The most polluted city depends on when you look.
04
Lived burden

How many days cross the WHO daily guideline?

The dot matrix changes the question from “what is the average?” to “how often do people experience above-guideline days?”

05
Particle profile

Bad air is not one single condition.

PM2.5 and PM10 describe different particle-size ranges. The PM2.5/PM10 ratio gives a particle-profile indicator, not direct source attribution.

06
Weather episodes

Weather context changes the shape of a bad day.

Wind speed, PM10, and particle ratio help distinguish high-PM2.5 days from PM10-heavy or coarse-particle-influenced episodes.

07
City fingerprints

Cities cluster by behavior, not just pollution level.

The fingerprint map compresses pollution burden, exceedance, seasonality, particle profile, and weather into one exploratory view.

08
Dubai deep dive

Inside one city, timing still matters.

Dubai is the local case study. The hour-by-month heatmap shows that even a city-level average hides daily timing and seasonal rhythm.

Figure: Dubai’s diurnal pollution fingerprint. Darker cells show higher median PM2.5 by local hour and month.

Final takeaways

Three things the city average hides.

Rankings are unstable.

City positions shift month by month, so annual rankings hide seasonal dynamics and short pollution episodes.

Exceedance burden matters.

The number of days above the WHO PM2.5 guideline better represents lived exposure than a single mean value.

Cities have fingerprints.

PM2.5 burden, PM10 context, weather, seasonality, and daily episodes create distinct pollution profiles.

Limitations

The analysis is descriptive and coverage-aware. PM2.5/PM10 ratios are interpreted as particle-profile indicators, not as direct source attribution. Sensor coverage differs by city, and source confirmation would require additional land-use, emissions, or source-apportionment data.
